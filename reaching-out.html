At HSLU’s Design, Film and Art school, ideas are currency. Whether you’re designing a new service, developing a product concept, or exploring a research-driven practice, the leap from concept to reality often feels daunting. That’s where Smart-up comes in. Smart-up is HSLU’s cross-school support programme for innovation, entrepreneurship and self-employment — and it’s available to every DFK student, free of charge.


What Smart-up Offers

Smart-up is not a business school programme. It’s a hands-on resource designed to meet you wherever your idea currently is — even if it’s just a hunch.
Free coaching (7 hours) Every student gets access to seven hours of individual coaching at no cost. The process starts with an introductory conversation where you discuss your project, its current status, and what kind of support would be most useful. From there, a personalised coaching plan is put together for you.
Expert referrals Coaches connect you with HSLU lecturers who have industry experience, as well as external specialists on topics like legal setup, funding, or market validation — depending on what your project needs.
Smart-up Package For projects that are ready to grow, the Smart-up Package offers 20 hours of coaching with internal and external experts, semi-annual site visits, visibility through HSLU’s blog, newsletter and social media, and even a business address within the school. The annual cost is CHF 120.
Workshops and events Throughout the year, Smart-up runs public workshops and networking events — from business validation and pitching to funding strategy. These are open to all HSLU students and are a low-barrier way to start engaging with the entrepreneurial community.
Infrastructure and co-working Smart-up hubs offer desk space and, for more established ventures, office rental within the university environment. Working alongside other student founders from different disciplines can be an unexpected source of collaborators and feedback.

Why This Matters for DFK Students

Design students are, by training, problem-solvers and systems thinkers — exactly the skills that make for strong founders. Many of the most compelling startups emerging from design schools are built on service concepts, speculative products, social impact tools, and creative platforms.
If your thesis project addresses a real problem, Smart-up can help you test whether it has a life beyond graduation. If you’re developing a freelance practice, coaching can help you structure your offer and understand your market. If you have a side project you’ve never quite formalised, this is the support structure to do it.
Smart-up also offers matchmaking — connecting teams looking for members with individuals who want to join a project. For DFK students with strong visual or interaction skills, this is a direct route to collaborating with students from Business, Engineering, or Computer Science on ventures that need a design lead.

Getting Started

  • Email smart-up@hslu.ch to register for an initial consultation — subject line: Anmeldung Erstgespräch
  • Attend a workshop or event to explore the ecosystem before committing — the events calendar is listed on the Smart-up page
  • Explore the Ideation Booklet at smart-up-map.ch — a free tool for systematically developing and testing a business concept
  • Check the DFK-specific Smart-up contact at the Lucerne School of Design, Film and Art Smart-up page for school-specific support

    • There’s no minimum viable idea required to reach out. Smart-up is designed for early stages — and the earlier you engage, the more useful it is.

    Short Introduction:

    Dr. -Ing. Martin Dressler is a faculty member and lecturer of the hslu master program. His area of expertise is in material design and engineering. He kindly agreed to be interviewed by us regarding his startup Spezialkeramik.ch GmbH.

    Question 01: Can you please explain what Spezialkeramik.ch does?

    Currently I do consulting for external clients in the field of material and technology while also developing my products. I am working on very thin ceramic plates that I can shape however a customer would need it.

    Question 02: How does funding of a startup work?

    There are basically three ways to fund a startup:

    1. External investors
      If you do so, you basically sell most of your company already to the external investor. And the amount of money you can acquire from an external investor obviously depends on your company.
    2. You have the money yourself
      You do not need to worry about investors because you have rich parents.
    3. You start out very small
      You offer a service that is already well established on the market, in my case it is consulting and with that money, you can support your startup.

    Question 03: How did it all start?

    I always had the feeling that I wanted to do something for myself. I wanted to be in the driver's seat. And obviously when you finish your degree, you don't have the funds, and you don't have the experience to do this. And I'm almost 50 now and I reached a point in my life where I realized, I know quite a bit already. Not everything, but I learned a lot and I gained enough to know the ceramics and high-tech materials market. I also established a few connections. Also, when you work already a couple of years, you had the chance to save some money. The final push was when I was invited to come over to Switzerland by a startup. So, I joined another startup and during that time I learned a lot also how to run a startup. After a time we went our separate ways and I started my own practice in the kitchen with a novel technology. From there I found a cheap workshop and acquired more equipment. In the first two years there is no income at all, so in order to have a stable income I also started a part-time job and got funds from my pension scheme.

    Question 4: How did you get more clients?

    Taking clients on board is a delicate thing to do if you are working with a novel technology. I'm always comparing this to opening a pizzeria. The process for making pizza is well known. Of course, you need to practice and hone the skills to make the perfect pizza Margherita. And the business model is also known: You have a restaurant, you make pizza, which tastes good, which is delicious and you know there is a market for pizza. What you have to do is deliver good pizza, if you don’t you will not have clients.

    Going back to spezialkeramik. My product is well known so you could compare it to the pizza. I am using a new technology, so it makes the ceramic or pizza better. But to achieve this, I need more development work. This kind of work is super hard to anticipate. You might be lucky and after two years you master the new pizza flavour or it could take five years or even longer. In an ideal case a client sees the importance of the development and supports the process. But this is like winning the lottery. You might want to approach one or two clients yourself, don’t approach ten! And they agree to buy a few prototypes with no guarantee that they fulfil all the criteria they want. That way you have pilot customer that expect not too much of the product but can test it. When I say selling products, I mean that I am paying most of it. But I have some insights in how to develop further. The worst thing you could possibly do is to be too fast with advertising for your product. That's also the reason why on my webpage I don't have any ceramic products so far, because if you start advertising and you go public with your product, it needs to be a product and not a prototype. There is a huge difference between prototype and product.

    Question 5: What is the difference between a product and a prototype?

    Some people get this wrong. They think a good idea or a first prototype is already a product. That is unfortunately not the case in an industrial setting. A product is something you can order and it has a defined quality and you can order the product repeatedly. As long as you have to tell a customer or a client, that it worked a week before and we're not sure if it works today or tomorrow, then it's not a product and you might end up in big trouble if you actually advertise your prototype as a product because some clients might not be as forgiving as your pilot clients. Your pilot clients might be forgiving because they get a discount rate. They know it's a prototype and they are part of the development process. But real-world clients, they want a product now and not any excuses.

    Question 6: How long did it take for you to go from prototype to a product?

    I am not yet at the product stage and that is the reason why I don't have any ceramic products on my web page. I have the consultancy. This is a product I can already sell.

    Question 7: How do you deal with work-life-balance?

    I was planning on getting the first product out this year. But I got sick and in April I was signed off work for almost a month. I basically came super close to a burnout. Because I have a part-time job, the teacher position at the hslu and a family with parents to take care of next to my startup. You have to be careful not to overdo it as I did. April was not a good month and also everything got delayed. But that’s what can happen in development work. It also turned out that there was still some instability in my products and the rejection rate is quite high and I have to keep working on this.

    Question 8: Apart from the first startup you worked with, are there other people involved?

    I don't have the money or the funds to sustain or to hire someone who could help me. And the keyword here is helping. If you are inventing or developing something new, this type of work does not scale with the amount of people who work on this process. To give a bit of a comparison, Einstein, he was working on the theory of relativity all by himself. Even if he had someone working with him creative leaps he took during his research work, which is also comparable to an engineering development group. It is not simply following an algorithm like do A, do B, do C all the way down to Z and then you have a development. By the way, this is also why I don't see any problem with large language models like ChatGPT. So essentially they all work with algorithms, but humans they do not and this is a huge advantage. And so during development work, I've realized during my whole career it is better in the beginning to work for yourself and to figure it out for yourself. It slows down the process, obviously, but it saves you headaches because when you involve other people in the development work, there's the social component coming into play. It's super rare that you meet someone who you get along with without any obstacles. So even between spouses, like husband and wife, there might be things which do not work. To have someone to start development work, you actually need someone closer than your spouse. Because it just slows down the process if you have to discuss or debate things all the time, if you cannot rely on someone, or if you even worse, if you have to be afraid that the other partner betrays you. So this is the no part, but the yes part is that I am working with people because you cannot be an expert in every field.

    Question 9: What skills or experiences would you say are crucial for a startup?

    One thing comes to my mind right away, and that is endurance. You have to be willing to go on a long journey and persist. It is no use working like crazy for a week and then go away for a month. This will not work because if you rent a workshop, the workshop is there. You have to pay a rent every month. If you're on vacation, yes or no. If you are sick, yes or no. Or if you're working 24/7, yes or no. You have to pay the rent every month. Also money and time for yourself is quite short. Another thing, you have to be critical with yourself and do not parade yourself. Listen to your gut if the product is ready or not. But this might be a personal thing, but it's still my fundamental belief. It's better to wait and to make sure your product is ready than to be fast. This would be the third thing: be modest, don’t parade yourself and take your time. The forth thing is the support of your family and friends.

    1. Endurance
    2. Be okay with little money and time for yourself
    3. Be modest
    4. Have a supporting family

    Question 10: Did you learn new skills, maybe that you didn't expect throughout your journey?

    Yes, I would say the business skills. Also negotiating skills, presentation skills and strategic thinking. I always considered myself someone who is capable of strategic thinking. By heart I am a perfectionist and I could go on forever, but I had to learn to limit myself. So this is something I had to learn: not losing myself in the details and staying on track.

    Question 11: When you say business skills, how, how did you learn or improve business skills?

    I come from a family that is employed, so I had to learn it myself. I have to pay attention to the small things because at the end they add up. Like packaging and small steps, and being honest how you work with your clients. You should not charge less because you're afraid a client might say no. You would be out of business really fast.

    Question 12: Would you say it is important to have this way of thinking in the beginning? Or first work on the idea and don’t have the numbers in your head?

    The business case in general should be an element right from the beginning, because eventually you are going to sell products. What I did was look at another competitor in Germany. I knew I could make better plates for less money. So I figured that is reasonable and I could actually go for the market price and be part of the game.

    Question 13: Are there situations where you made a mistake but could learn from it?

    A mistake I have witnessed in the hardware realm is to invest a lot of money in the beginning. You should be super careful with spending your hard-earned money and be 100% sure that piece of hardware or equipment are worth the money. There is also the optimization problem. Sometimes you save money but end up paying with speed, which is an optimization problem.

    Another thing I did wrong, was to overdo it with the workload. That is why I ended up with a burnout at the beginning of 2025. You have to prepare yourself for a marathon, it is not a sprint. When you have a startup, it can change your life. And working 16 hours a day with weekends is not sustainable.

    Question 14: did your PhD help you in your start up?

    It is not a clear no, but I know some people who have a PhD and did not actually contribute to the science world. On the other hand there are some super smart people who are working on very interesting projects, and they don’t have a PhD. So, I think it comes down to experience, expertise and knowledge and not a title.

    Question 15: How important are networking and networking events?

    No, for me it is too premature. So network events are better if you want to expand your company. I am not at the point to actually sell a product.

    Question 16: Is there anything you wished you knew before starting your startup?

    Technological knowledge, but at the same time it is part of a process and sometimes the solution is very simple but the journey was needed to reach that point. And for me definitely the health aspect could have been avoided. There is a quote by Marlene Dietrich, where she was asked the question: “Would you do everything the same in your life?” and she answered with: “Yes, especially the mistakes”.

    Question 17: What do you think makes a successful startup?

    That is quite easy: A successful startup is a venture which eventually provides a real world product on the market and which pays the bills. It is quite simple.

    Question 18: How do you use AI and does it help you in some way?

    I use it mainly for research. For example I need a solvent which has a certain evaporation rate and is more or less non-toxic, I would ask AI for a list. I think this is something where AI-models are super good at. I also asked AI once to make a leaflet. But I think it is not there yet. This is important for you designers: the tools are not creative, they are repetitive. Repeating a process is not a creative process.

    Question 19: Would you like to add anything?

    Yes, I think a common misconception about starting your own company is that you are earning a lot of money. But a startup is like an explorer. You have to keep in mind, you don’t start a startup to become rich. You want to begin a startup because you want to make something your own way or you want to make a difference. And if I happened to make a lot of money, I could quit my parttime jobs, except the one the the hslu. I really enjoy the feedback and refreshing ideas from you guys. If I made enough money one day, I think I would also buy a horse!

    What is InCube?

    InCube is Switzerland's largest student innovation challenge, organised by the ETH Entrepreneur Club. Teams of five participants from universities across the world spend five days living and working inside a glass cube in a public location, developing a solution to a real-world problem posed by a sponsoring company. The journey begins weeks earlier with a virtual kickoff (PrepCube) and a week-long retreat in the Swiss Alps (OutCube), where participants train in entrepreneurship, design thinking, and rapid prototyping before the main challenge. The finale is a live pitch in front of a jury of executives and an audience of around 500 students.

    Our Team & Challenge

    Our team was the Siemens cube, positioned at Polytherasse in Zurich. Our challenge, sponsored by Siemens, was “Talk to Your Building” - exploring how an AI system could understand and interact with a building’s data and controls in a human-friendly way. We developed a solution, Talk to Your Building, was an LLM-powered interface designed to make smart building systems accessible to visually impaired users. Instead of relying on screens or visual dashboards, occupants could interact with the building through natural language — asking questions, adjusting settings, and navigating the environment through voice-based AI. We won the main prize.

    Team Journey on Instagram

    We documented our process in real time on Instagram at @siemens.incube through the five days in the cube to the finale pitch. The account captures the team dynamic, prototyping sessions, and the build-up to the win.

    Why InCube is relevant to HSLU Master Design

    InCube is a direct extension of what the Master Design programme trains students for: working with ambiguous briefs, using design thinking under pressure, and prototyping for real stakeholders. Participating as a designer in a cross-disciplinary team, alongside engineers and business students — placed service design methods at the centre of a high-stakes innovation process. The challenge also connects to the programme's focus on socially meaningful design: our solution addressed accessibility and inclusion for visually impaired people in built environments, using emerging AI technology to close a real gap. For HSLU, participation in InCube demonstrates that its graduates operate credibly at the intersection of design, technology, and business in one of Switzerland's most competitive student innovation contexts.
